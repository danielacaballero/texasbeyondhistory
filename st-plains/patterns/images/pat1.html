About 2-3,000 years ago, antler racks from white-tail deer were carefully placed over certain burials, perhaps as a signature of status or other symbolic distinction. This deer antler cluster was recovered in a grave at Loma Sandia along with deer skull fragments, chipped stone tools, flakes, and a mano (hand stone used for grinding). Whether the antler rack was originally used as a headdress (such as that depicted in the painting) could not be determined in this case because of the deterioration of the bone. Some burials at other cemeteries were covered by many racks.  Given that deer was the number one big game animal hunted in the region throughout most of prehistory (as it still is today), these animals must have symbolized success.  It is interesting to note that antler rack offerings sometimes accompanied adult men, obviously the main hunters, but they also have been found with women and children. This suggests the ritual links were not as simple as honoring hunting success.   Photo courtesy of UTSA-Center for Archaeological Research.
