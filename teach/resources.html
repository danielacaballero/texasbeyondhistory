Texas Beyond History

The stories of people and places of Texas past, as revealed by archeology, come alive in the Texas Beyond History website. In the Site Exhibits section (main Texas map), students can learn about a 1000-year-old Indian village, or rancheria, on the banks of the Llano River (the Graham-Applegate site), or thrill to images of hundreds of bison driven to their death over an 80-foot cliff in the Lower Pecos (Bonfire Shelter). There is the courageous story of a freed slave family in Austin at the turn of the twentieth century (Rubin Hancock), and the haunting tale of a little-known prisoner of war camp for Union soldiers in East Texas (Camp Ford).

Breathtaking images of extinct big-game animals, stalked by the earliest hunters in Texas, enliven the pages of the Lubbock Lake exhibit. At the Alibates site in the Texas Panhandle, students can learn about a brilliantly hued stone—Alibates flint—that has been quarried to make stone tools for nearly 13,000 years. French fur traders exchanged incredible beads, flintlock weapons, and other goods with an enterprising group of Native Americans in the seventeenth century at what is now known as the Gilbert site. These are just a few of the sites now on-line, and these will be updated and added to in the future.

Clicking on any of the site locations on the main page map of Texas will cause a thumbnail description to pop up as well as a timeline indicating the site's age. There is also an extensive Glossary with definitions and colorful accompanying images to help explain unknown words. There are also special Kids pages with lively games, puzzles, and pictures, including a brainy, trowel-wielding armadillo (Dr. Dirt) who answers questions about archeology.
