READING AND DOING LESSONS

Some of the artifacts found at the farmstead are related to learning how to read and write. These items are evidence that the Williams children were receiving an education, perhaps by traveling to the school at nearby Manchaca or the Antioch colony.

Archeologists determined from looking at old records that Ransom and Sarah did not know how to read or write. However, they obviously valued the idea of education and did what they could to make sure their children were literate.

African-American newspapers in nearby Austin also stressed the importance of education, particularly for emancipated slaves and their children. One newspaper editor even published the alphabet for children to use in their studies at home.

The editor of The Gold Dollar, the first African-American newspaper in Austin, published the alphabet to encourage education in black families. This issue is from the 1870s.
Fragments of slate pencils, used to mark on writing slates.

In the 1800s, many children learned to write using slates and slate pencils. Because paper and pencils were relatively expensive, students often did their daily classwork and practiced skills such as handwriting and math on a framed piece of black slate., known as a slate table. The teacher or an older child would check the work, then the student could erase the assignment and begin the next one.

Archeologist found two large pieces of broken writing slates and several pieces of slate pencils at the farm. One of the slate fragments has some symbols on it that were engraved with a knife blade or other sharp-pointed object. Slate pencils were made of a soft rock like talc or soapstone to practice writing.

This broken writing slate saw lots of use on the Williams farm; it is marked with scribbles made by a sharp instrument. Can you read any of the letters or symbols?
These pencil erasors and leads from the farm are more familiar to students today.

Several pencil erasers and bits of graphite pencil leads from wooden pencils also were found at the farm site. These writing utensils are more familiar to modern school kids. One specimen has its original gum eraser and part of the wooden pencil still encased within the ferrule. Another type of writing tool found at the farm were carbon battery rods that had been sharpened on both ends. These may have worked just like pencils, leaving a heavy black mark on surfaces.
Slate tables typically would have been enclosed in a wood frame to help protect the slate, as shown in this photo.

The Williams children may have done their studies in the evening on the porch, but in the winter they may have worked by lamplight inside the cabin. Archeologists found a variety of lamp parts at the farm site, including the burner mechanisms. These sections rested on top of the base that held the kerosene or other oil that was burned to make light. These pieces probably came from a lantern that once had a glass globe held in place by a metal frame. No rural homes had electricity in the late 1800’s, so lanterns were the only source of light in the evening.

This brass burner plate was part of a lantern that provided light for the Williams' family.
