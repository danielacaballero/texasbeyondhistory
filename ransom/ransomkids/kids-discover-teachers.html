Teachers’ Guide to Student Educational Activities:
 Discover a 19th-Century African American Farmstead

Ransom and Sarah Williams were former slaves who raised 9 children on their farm southwest of Austin, Texas. We know about their lives from archeological and historical research conducted at the site in 2008-2010. The five activities in this module provide an opportunity for students to learn what life was like for an African-American family after Emancipation and in the early years of the 20th century. These should be particularly valuable and interesting activities for Black History Month.

A handout of questions to test your students’ knowledge of these activities is included below and may be downloaded as a pdf file. A standards-based, multi-day lesson for 4th-7th grades also is correlated to this activity set (see www.texasbeyondhistory.net/ransom/teach.html). Here's what's covered in the activities:

Activity 1: What’s the Story is a colorful and fun interactive led by Dr. Dirt, the Armadillo Archeologist.  He explains why the farmstead site and the Williams family are significant and how archeologists and historians wove together evidence about their lives.

Activity 2: In Explore the Farm, students examine a special interactive painting of the Williams Family in 1878. In the process, they will learn about individual family members and the artifacts that helped archeologists reconstruct these engaging scenes of farm and family life, from working in the fields to playing with toys.

Activity 3: Peek Inside the Cabin works off a scene of Sarah Williams and her daughter Mattie engaged in a typical activity after supper. Students are challenged to find “hidden clues” to several fascinating artifacts found inside the cabin.

Activity 4: In Check out the Corral, students see the various types of evidence that archeologists used to reconstruct the corral area on the farm and how they learned  that Ransom Williams raised horses.

Activity 5: Visit on the Porch is an imaginary story about the Williams family woven together from the actual evidence recovered in farmstead investigations.  Teachers may read it aloud to the class or students may read independently.
