Meet an Archeologist

Dr. Maria Franklin
Associate Professor in Anthropology, University of Texas at Austin


I spent my childhood in Germany, where my family took long road trips to historic sites like castles and old churches. That’s what got me interested in history. By the time I was in college and wondering what I should do, I decided to do something that I loved. Choosing to be an archeologist was easy. I thought it would be fun to spend my days outside at a site digging in the dirt and finding artifacts, puzzling through what happened there, and learning who the people were who once lived there.


 My very first excavation was on a Creek Indian site called Fusihatchee in Alabama, where Native Americans had lived for thousands of years. These days I do archeology in Texas, and study the African Americans who were enslaved until the end of the Civil War. I want to know about their family and community life, and how they survived as farmers during a time when racism and inequality was common for African Americans.

 

 I also teach archeology at the University of Texas, and I enjoy involving my students in my research. I think it’s important for young Americans to know their history, and to give them the chance to learn how we can discover the past through the artifacts that people once used. Check out the Ransom and Sara Williams Farmstead site, where my students and I worked with other archeologists to learn about how the Williams family lived during the time of Reconstruction in Texas.
Here I am working on the site of an African-American farmstead near Austin, built in the 1870s.
