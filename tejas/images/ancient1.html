Harleton Appliquéd Jar
Taylor Site (41HS3)
ca. 1430-1680 AD

Height = 10cm
Mouth Diameter = 13.8 cm

The decorations on the vessel were made before the pot was fired. The appliqués were made from clay fillets that were attached while the clay was still malleable. These were applied in the shape of concentric diamonds. The punctations on the neck of the vessel, and the hatches on the appliqués were made with a rigid tool while the pot was still "green." That is after it was hardened, but before it was fired.

This elaborately decorated jar was found in a cemetery at the H. R. Taylor Farm in Harrison County, Texas in the summer of 1931 by a University of Texas excavation crew under the direction of archeologist A. T. Jackson. The jar was found with four additional pots and 12 arrow points all tightly clustered together on the north side of Burial A55. Based on the grave offerings and the size of the pit, the excavator thought this was probably the grave of a youth, although no skeletal material was present. The presence of the 12 arrow points, however, could indicate that it may have been an adult male.
